Linear Transform#

A production of a vector from the right side of a m×nm\times nm×n (row-column number) matrix AAA defined on a field FFF is a linear transformation

A:Fn→C(A)⊂Fm,A:\mathbb{F}^n\to \mathcal{C}(A)\subset\mathbb{F}^m,A:Fn→C(A)⊂Fm,

where C(A)\mathcal{C}(A)C(A) is the column space of AAA. Since the matrix can be expressed as A=(a1,…,an)A=(\boldsymbol{a}_1,\dots,\boldsymbol{a}_n)A=(a1​,…,an​), and the transform AxA\boldsymbol{x}Ax can be epxressed as

Ax=∑i=1nxiai,x=(x1,…,xn).A\boldsymbol{x}=\sum_{i=1}^nx_i\boldsymbol{a}_i,\quad x=(x1,\dots, x_n).Ax=i=1∑n​xi​ai​,x=(x1,…,xn​).
  • The null space of AAA, denoted as Null(A)\text{Null}(A)Null(A) is the space that Ax=0A\boldsymbol{x}=0Ax=0, ∀x∈Null(A)\forall \boldsymbol{x}\in\text{Null}(A)∀x∈Null(A). It is a complementary space of the column space of AAA
    Fn=C(A)+Null(A∗).\mathbb{F}^n=\mathcal{C}(A)+\text{Null}(A^*).Fn=C(A)+Null(A∗).
Isomorphism

The linear transformation is also known as homomorphism. And it is isomorphism if the dimension of Null(A)\text{Null}(A)Null(A) is 0.

Production of Matrices#

The production of two matrix Am×nA_{m\times n}Am×n​ and Bn×hB_{n\times h}Bn×h​ is defined as

Cij=∑k=1nAikBkj,C_{ij}=\sum_{k=1}^{n}A_{ik}B_{kj},Cij​=k=1∑n​Aik​Bkj​,

so that CCC is a m×hm\times hm×h matrix. It is obviously that C(C)⊂C(A)\mathcal{C}(C)\subset \mathcal{C}(A)C(C)⊂C(A). On the other, if we represent

A=(a1,…,ak),C=(c1,…,ck),A=(\boldsymbol{a}_1,\dots,\boldsymbol{a}_k), \quad C=(\boldsymbol{c}_1,\dots,\boldsymbol{c}_k),A=(a1​,…,ak​),C=(c1​,…,ck​),

then C(C)⊂C(A)\mathcal{C}(C)\subset \mathcal{C}(A)C(C)⊂C(A) means that ci=∑kbikak\boldsymbol{c}_i=\sum_{k}b_{ik}\boldsymbol{a}_kci​=∑k​bik​ak​ so that bikb_{ik}bik​ forms a matrix that AB=CAB=CAB=C. It leads to the theorem that for a two matrix AAA and CCC, C(C)⊂C(A)\mathcal{C}(C)\subset \mathcal{C}(A)C(C)⊂C(A) if and only if there exists a matrix BBB such that AB=CAB=CAB=C.

Invariant Space and Eigenvectors#

A invariant vector space VVV for linear transform Am×mA_{m\times m}Am×m​ is a space that Ax∈VA\boldsymbol{x}\in VAx∈V for all x∈V\boldsymbol{x}\in Vx∈V. In the case that the invariant space dimension is 1, it means that any vectors v∈V\boldsymbol{v}\in Vv∈V can only be scaled by a constant factor after the transformation: Av=λvA\boldsymbol{v}=\lambda \boldsymbol{v}Av=λv. Those vectors are called eigenvectors with a eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ. The eigenvalues of a matrix AAA is determined by the equation

det⁡(A−λIm)=0.\det(A-\lambda I_m)=0.det(A−λIm​)=0.

Eigenvectors with distinct eigenvalues are linear independent.

The set of all eigenvalues of AAA is also known as the spectra of AAA.

Theorems:
  1. The existence of the solution of the eigen-equation is determined by the properties of the field F\mathbb{F}F where AAA is defined. For a algebra complete field such as C\mathbb{C}C, the eigenvalues are always exists for any square matrix AAA. This can be rephrasing into the form that any linear operators defined on C\mathcal{C}C (or R\mathcal{R}R) will have 1-dimensional (2-dimensional) invariant subspace.
  2. Eigenvectors exists for any symmetric matrix AAA defined on R\mathbb{R}R.

The 2nd theorem is a strength version of the 1st theorem for real symmetric matrices. Based on the 1st theorem, it is guaranteed that 2-dimensional invariant spaces LLL exist for AAA and it could be expressed as

AL=(abbc).A_L=\left( \begin{array}{cc} a & b\\ b & c \end{array} \right).AL​=(ab​bc​).

For the eigenvalue equation (a−λ)(c−λ)−b2=0(a-\lambda)(c-\lambda)-b^2=0(a−λ)(c−λ)−b2=0, we have (a−c)2+4b2≥0(a-c)^2+4b^2\ge 0(a−c)2+4b2≥0 which means that real solutions exists for the det⁡(AL)\det(A_L)det(AL​) so that this 2-dimensional invariant space contained two 1-dimensional invariant spaces.

Geometric interpretation

A transform defined by a m×mm\times mm×m matrix is combination of projections, rotations, coordinate scaling and reflection. The eigenvector defined the direction of the rotation axis and the eigenvalues defined the coordinate scaling factor along this direction.

Singularity Value Decomposition#

Given a matrix Am×nA_{m\times n}Am×n​ defined over a field F\mathbb{F}F, a singular value σ≥0\sigma\ge 0σ≥0 corresponds to a pair of unit vector v∈Fn\boldsymbol{v}\in\mathbb{F}^nv∈Fn and u∈Fm\boldsymbol{u}\in\mathbb{F}^mu∈Fm such that

Av=σu,A∗u=σv,A\boldsymbol{v}= \sigma \boldsymbol{u},\quad A^*\boldsymbol{u}=\sigma\boldsymbol{v},Av=σu,A∗u=σv,

where v\boldsymbol{v}v and u\boldsymbol{u}u is known as right-singular and left-singular vector for AAA. This decomposition is known as singular value decomposition (SVD).

Given a m×nm\times nm×n matrix defined on a field F\mathcal{F}F, it can be decomposed into the form

A=VΣW∗,A=V\Sigma W^*,A=VΣW∗,

where the VVV and WWW are m×mm\times mm×m and n×nn\times nn×n unitary matrix consists by left and right singular vectors, respectively. The Σ\SigmaΣ is a m×nm\times nm×n matrix where the diagonal elements are singular values and the rests are 0.

The connection between the SVD and ED. Say the SVD of AAA is A=VΣW∗A=V\Sigma W^*A=VΣW∗, then we have AA∗=VΣW∗WΣV∗=VΣΣ∗V∗AA^*=V\Sigma W^*W\Sigma V^*=V\Sigma\Sigma^*V^*AA∗=VΣW∗WΣV∗=VΣΣ∗V∗. On the other hand, Since AA∗AA^*AA∗ or A∗AA^*AA∗A is symmetric, we have the ED AA∗=VΛ1V∗AA^*=V\Lambda_1 V^*AA∗=VΛ1​V∗ which shows that ΣΣ∗=Λ1\Sigma\Sigma^*=\Lambda_1ΣΣ∗=Λ1​. The same for A∗A=W∗ΣΣ∗WA^*A=W^*\Sigma\Sigma^* WA∗A=W∗ΣΣ∗W such that Σ∗Σ=Λ2\Sigma^*\Sigma=\Lambda_2Σ∗Σ=Λ2​. Without lose generality, we assume that m≥nm\ge nm≥n then we have

Σ=(Λn0(m−n)×n),\Sigma=\left( \begin{array}{c} \sqrt{\Lambda_n} \\ 0_{(m-n)\times n} \end{array} \right),Σ=(Λn​​0(m−n)×n​​),

where Λn\sqrt{\Lambda}_nΛ​n​ is the n×nn\times nn×n diagonal matrix with square root of the eigenvalues as diagonal elements.
